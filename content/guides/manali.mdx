## Intro

I have lived in Old Manali since 2018. I run manali.today, where I post live snow, Atal Tunnel, and Rohtang Pass status, because tourists message local people every single day asking the same three questions. This guide is everything else people ask me. Not the version you get from a travel agency that wants to sell you a fixed itinerary, and not the version some blog wrote without ever sleeping a night here. Just what I would tell a friend who said "we're coming to Manali, help."

## Getting to Manali

Most people arrive by road. [Manali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manali,_Himachal_Pradesh) sits at the northern end of the Kullu Valley, roughly 530 to 550 km from Delhi, and the drive takes around 10 to 12 hours depending on traffic and weather, usually via Chandigarh, Bilaspur, Mandi, and Kullu. From Chandigarh alone it's about 280 to 310 km, roughly 6 to 7 hours. There's no direct train. The nearest railheads are Chandigarh and Kalka, both still several hours away by road. If you'd rather fly, Bhuntar Airport (also called Kullu-Manali Airport) is about 50 km south, with a scenic hour and a half drive left to reach Manali. Flights from Delhi exist but are limited and weather dependent, especially in winter.

The new highway stretch after Bilaspur is genuinely worth knowing about if you're driving yourself. It's smooth, modern, and runs through a long sequence of tunnels and bridges, a real upgrade over what the road used to be. The patch that still catches people out is between Pandoh and the Aut tunnel, particularly around the Pandoh-Takoli tunnel and the Pandoh bypass project, both still under construction. Expect slower going and possible queuing through that specific stretch regardless of season.

If you're driving yourself, know that the road narrows and gets winding past Mandi, fuel stations thin out, and during peak season the final stretch into Manali can crawl. Fuel up before the hills start. Keep cash on you. Smaller dhabas and shops along the way don't always take cards.

## Best Time to Visit, in Short

I'm going to keep this section brief because it deserves its own full guide, which I'm working on. Quick version: March to June is generally pleasant and the most popular window, which also means the most traffic. September to November gives you post-monsoon clarity and noticeably fewer people. December to February is for snow, but road access can get unpredictable, [check current snow status](/) before you commit to a winter trip. Avoid the July to August monsoon window if you can, landslides do happen on the Chandigarh-Manali highway most years.

| Window | What it's good for | What to watch for |
|---|---|---|
| March to June | Pleasant weather, all roads typically open, school holiday season | Heaviest crowds and traffic, book stays well ahead |
| July to August | Lush green valley, fewer foreign tourists | Monsoon landslide risk on the highway, Rohtang access unreliable |
| September to November | Clear skies, post-monsoon visibility, calmer | Days get shorter and colder by late November |
| December to February | Snow in town and around, the quietest local feel | Some roads close with snowfall, dress for genuine cold |

## Where You Actually Stay Matters More Than Your Itinerary

Every year I watch families spend a serious amount of money to come here, only to get stuck in traffic and chaos for most of the trip. It genuinely makes me sad, because I know what that money represents. A year of saving. A year of managing work stress around two weeks off. Summer is often the only window families get. Kids have school holidays then. Grandparents can't handle the cold of a winter visit. So when people online tell families not to visit during peak season because of traffic photos, they're ignoring that not everyone has the privilege of visiting whenever they want.

So here's my actual advice. Avoid tour agencies if you can help it, they hand your whole trip over to someone else's schedule and someone else's commission incentives. Book a good place to stay directly, and if you're arriving by bus, just ask them to arrange a pickup. Avoid staying in Nasogi, Mall Road, or Siyal, these areas are convenient on paper but mean you're in the thick of the congestion the entire trip.

Instead, look at Shanag, Burwa, Jagatsukh, or upper Naggar. Before you book anywhere, check if there's a meadow nearby, or a short, easy hike. You'll have a far better trip spending unhurried time with your family somewhere beautiful than trying to tick off a checklist while stuck in traffic.

A bit on each of these, since "stay somewhere quieter" isn't that useful without specifics. Shanag and Burwa sit on the slopes above and across the valley, both quiet, both still close enough to drive into town for a meal or some shopping without living in the middle of it. Old Manali itself, where I live, is busier than Shanag or Burwa but calmer than Mall Road, with its own cafe culture and river walk, more on that below.

Jagatsukh is the one most tourists drive straight past, which is a mistake. It was the original capital of the Kullu kingdom, under its old name Nast, where the earliest Rajas ruled for about twelve generations before the seat moved to Naggar. A few of its old temples still stand, including a Shiva temple in the Shikhara style with genuinely fine stone carving. Staying here puts you close to the Hamta trail and Jobra without any of the Manali town crowd.

Naggar, a little further down the valley, was the Kullu kingdom's capital after Jagatsukh, for close to two centuries, before the capital moved again to present-day Kullu town. [Naggar Castle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naggar,_Himachal_Pradesh) still stands from that period, built in the Kathkuni style using interlocking deodar wood beams and stone with no mortar, which is part of why it survived a major earthquake in 1905 that flattened most other buildings in the valley. The Russian painter Nicholas Roerich settled in Naggar in the early twentieth century and his old home is now a small gallery. It's a genuinely peaceful base if you want distance from the chaos without giving up culture and history on your doorstep.

Bring a storybook or a board game. Read a little about who actually lived and studied in this valley, Manu, Vyas, Vashisht, Jamadagni, Kashyap, Parashar. Manali's own name comes from "Manu-Alaya," meaning the abode of [Manu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manu_(Hinduism)), the sage who in Hindu tradition is said to have stepped off his ark here to begin human life again after a great flood. The Kullu Valley itself is called the Valley of the Gods. Tell your kids that story while you're here. It lands differently than reading it at home, with the actual mountains the story is set in right outside the window.

## The Tourist Checklist, Honestly Reviewed

These are the places every tour guide will put on your itinerary. Here's how I'd actually rate them if you're visiting in peak season.

**Atal Tunnel.** Genuinely great views and a smooth ride, but during peak season it's not worth the time cost. You can lose 10 to 12 hours round trip from Manali to Sissu and back in traffic. I'd skip it unless you have real flexibility in your schedule. [Check live Atal Tunnel status](/is-atal-tunnel-open-today) before you commit either way. If you do go, do not just take the U-turn at Sissu and head back. Push on to Gondhla village and see the roughly 400-year-old fort there. It's ruined and abandoned, but worth the short detour, and worth reading about beforehand.

**Rohtang Pass.** Not worth it if you've already experienced snow somewhere else. If snow itself is the goal, a winter visit gives you a much better experience of it than the peak season scramble. [Check the live Rohtang Pass status](/is-rohtang-pass-open-today) before you plan around it, conditions change fast up there.

**[Hidimba Devi Temple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hidimba_Devi_Temple) (Manu Temple).** Worth it if you specifically want to seek blessings, the temple is genuinely significant, built in the 16th century in a striking wooden pagoda style set among tall cedar trees. Not worth standing in long queues purely for sightseeing during the busiest hours. Go early morning instead.

**The Manali Club House.** A hard no for most people, unless you specifically want to play snooker, table tennis, pool, or carrom with your family as an activity in itself.

**Solang Valley.** Worth including if anyone in your group wants to paraglide, the valley sits at around 2,560 m, about 13 to 14 km from Manali, with the main paragliding launch around 2,400 to 2,600 m depending on the operator. Go early, ideally before 9 AM, both to beat the crowd and because wind conditions are usually better earlier in the day. In winter it switches over to a small skiing setup. I'm working on a dedicated Solang guide with the operator and pricing detail, this section is just to say it's worth half a day if adventure activities matter to your group, and skippable if they don't.

If I were bringing my own family, here's exactly what I'd do. Find a stay near Shanag or Burwa. Take everyone for a picnic toward the nearest meadow. Visit Hidimba early in the morning, we're Shakti worshippers, so that matters to us personally, or spend that slot on Mall Road for some shopping instead. That's a full, calm day, and it's enough.

## Hidden Places Worth the Detour

A few spots that don't make the standard itinerary, but should.

Take the trail going up from Jagatsukh toward Bhanara village. Find a stay up there and walk to the Nag Devta temple, one of the best hidden spots in the valley. From the temple you can continue a little further to a small meadow called Baghi, surrounded by forest on all sides. It's hard to describe how good this place is, you have to see it. One important note: don't wander that area after sunset, there are bears around.

If you want to get away from the chaos entirely, look at Sethan. The road up is steep and not particularly well maintained, so it's not for every vehicle, but the views and meadows are worth it. Local lore holds that the Pandavas spent part of their exile, their agyatvas, in this village.

## Old Manali, Where I Actually Live

If you head into Old Manali, I want to give a specific mention to Krishna Dhaba, a small family-run local cafe with honest prices and a genuinely lovely owner. It's where a lot of the long-term Old Manali community hangs out daily. I do some of my work on manali.today from there, so there's a real chance you'll see me around if you stop by.

If you're looking for day hikes from Old Manali, here are three I'd actually recommend:

The Kharma Valley trail starts from the alley near the temple parking by Rama Dhaba. It turns into a trail heading down toward the river and a small water station, a good spot to just sit and cool off. Keep going up instead, and you'll reach Suicide Point, a name that undersells the place, it's a striking, large cracked rock hanging off the hillside.

From Rocky's Cafe, a trail heads up through three meadows, one after another, each a little higher and quieter than the last.

Or take the right-hand trail instead of the left from Rocky's, and you'll head toward Goshal village, with good stopping points along the way.

## A Little History, While You're Here

Manali's name itself carries the story. "Manu-Alaya," the abode of Manu, the lawgiver and sage of Hindu cosmology, said to have resettled humanity here after a great flood. The Kullu Valley around it is called the Valley of the Gods for a reason, this entire region threads through the lives of sages mentioned across Hindu tradition. Temples here, like the 16th-century Hidimba Devi Temple, sit on top of much older layers of belief and memory.

What most visitors don't realize is that several of the sages tied to this valley are also credited as founders of the six classical schools of Hindu philosophy, the darshanas, that still shape Indian thought today. I find this worth knowing before you walk through these hills, not because you need to be religious, but because it changes how you see the place.

Sage Kapila is traditionally credited as the founder of [Samkhya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samkhya), generally considered the oldest of the six schools, built around the idea that everything in reality stems from two principles, purusha (consciousness) and prakriti (matter). Patanjali built on Samkhya's framework to found [Yoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoga_(philosophy)) philosophy, less the physical postures most people associate with the word today, and more a systematic eight limbed path toward stilling the mind. Sage Gautama, sometimes called Akshapada, founded [Nyaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nyaya), a school built almost entirely on logic and valid reasoning, asking how we can actually know anything at all. Sage Kanada's [Vaisheshika](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaisheshika) grew out of Nyaya and proposed something startling for its time, that the physical universe is built from indivisible atoms, a kind of ancient Indian physics. Sage Jaimini founded [Mimamsa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81), concerned with ritual, duty, and the correct interpretation of Vedic text. And Vyasa, the same sage credited with compiling the Mahabharata, is traditionally linked to [Vedanta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vedanta), the school that asks what is ultimately real, and whether the self and that ultimate reality are one and the same.

You don't need to study any of this to enjoy a trip here. But the next time you're sitting in a meadow above Jagatsukh or Naggar, it's worth remembering that the ground under your feet has been written about and argued over by people trying to answer the biggest questions humans ask, for a very long time. That context changes a holiday into something with a bit more weight.

## Practical Basics

Manali sits at about 2050m, noticeably higher than most Indian plains cities, so don't be surprised if you feel a little more breathless than usual on arrival, especially if you're headed further up to Rohtang (around 3,980m) or even Solang's paragliding launch points. If anyone in your group has a heart or respiratory condition, it's worth reading up on [altitude sickness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altitude_sickness) before you go, and talking to a doctor beforehand if there's any doubt, rather than figuring it out on the mountain.

Mobile network is reliable in town but patches out fast once you're in the side valleys like Jagatsukh, Naggar, or anywhere past Solang, plan around that rather than relying on live navigation everywhere. ATMs exist in the main market but can run out of cash during peak season weekends, carry more cash than you think you'll need, especially if you're staying somewhere quieter like Shanag or Burwa where card payments are rare. Pack layers regardless of season, mountain weather changes faster than forecasts suggest, and evenings get cold here even in the middle of summer.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is Manali good for a family trip in peak summer season?**
Yes, but only if you plan around the traffic rather than against it. Stay somewhere quieter like Shanag or Burwa instead of Mall Road or Nasogi, and pick two or three things to actually do well instead of trying to cover everything on a fixed tour itinerary.

**Is it worth visiting Rohtang Pass and Atal Tunnel in the same trip?**
Not usually in peak season. Both can cost most of a day each in traffic. Pick one based on what you actually want, snow experience for Rohtang, or the tunnel drive and Gondhla fort detour for Atal Tunnel.

**How many days do I need in Manali?**
Four to five days is enough to see Old Manali, take one day trip, and still have unhurried time, rather than rushing between checklist stops.

**Do I need a permit for Atal Tunnel?**
No, the tunnel itself doesn't require a permit. [Rohtang Pass](/is-rohtang-pass-open-today) does. Check the live [Atal Tunnel](/is-atal-tunnel-open-today) and Rohtang Pass status pages on this site before you plan your day around either one.
