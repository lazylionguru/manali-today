I see it every day from spring through autumn. Cars line up at Atal Tunnel before dawn. They crawl through, emerge on the other side, and most of them drive straight to Sissu. They take a photo by the waterfall, maybe eat something, then turn around and fight the traffic back to Manali. Ten to twelve hours round trip in peak season, and they saw maybe two things.

There's a place past Sissu that almost nobody stops for. Gondhla village. And up above it, a fort that's been sitting there for roughly four hundred years, mostly forgotten.

## The Trail, Start to Finish

You cross Atal Tunnel heading north. That's the easy part if you [check live Atal Tunnel status](https://manali.today/is-atal-tunnel-open-today) before you leave. The tunnel saves you the Rohtang Pass climb, but it doesn't save you from the traffic on the other side. Sissu is maybe twenty minutes past the tunnel exit on a good day. On a bad day, double that.

Most people stop at Sissu. Don't. Push on past the Sissu lake, past the cluster of cafes and the waterfall crowds. The road continues along the Chenab River valley, and the landscape opens up. The mountains get bigger. The traffic thins out.

Gondhla village is about 15 kilometers past Sissu. You'll see the fort before you see the village. It sits on a ridge above the road, stone walls crumbling into the hillside. There's a rough track leading up from the main road, but you'll want to park near the village and walk. The path is steep in places, loose stone underfoot. It takes maybe twenty minutes from the road to the fort walls, depending on your pace and how many times you stop to look back at the valley.

The fort itself is ruined. Roofs gone in most sections. Walls cracked and leaning. Some rooms still hold their shape, and you can walk through them. The entrance arch stands, though part of it has collapsed. There's no restoration work happening. No ticket booth. No signboards explaining what you're looking at. Just the old stone and the wind.

You can climb to the highest remaining section. From there you see the whole valley. The Chenab River below. The highway threading through. The village houses with their slate roofs. And across the valley, more mountains, snow on the higher peaks even in summer.

## What Makes It Worth Doing

The fort is roughly four hundred years old. Nobody knows exactly who built it, though local stories say it belonged to a Raja from the Kullu side. The architecture is different from the forts you see in other parts of Himachal. Thicker walls, smaller windows, a design meant for defense rather than display. You can see where the watchtowers were, where the main gate would have been reinforced. For context on the broader history of hill forts in the region, you can read about [forts in Himachal Pradesh on Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category:Forts_in_Himachal_Pradesh).

What makes it worth the extra drive is the quiet. Sissu in peak season is loud. Buses idling. People shouting over each other at the food stalls. Gondhla is the opposite. I've been up to the fort maybe a dozen times since 2018. I've never seen another tourist there. A few local kids sometimes, shepherds passing through, but mostly I've had the place to myself.

The fort is also a good place to read about before you go. The history isn't well documented online, but there are a few academic papers and old travel accounts that mention it. The roughly 400-year-old fort there is ruined and abandoned but worth the short detour and worth reading about beforehand. You'll appreciate the layout more if you know what you're walking through.

And the light. Late afternoon is best. The sun drops behind the western ridge and the stone turns gold. The shadows get long. The wind picks up. It feels like a place that time forgot, because it basically is.

## Practical Notes

Timing matters. Atal Tunnel itself in peak season can cost 10 to 12 hours round trip from Manali to Sissu and back in traffic. Adding Gondhla means you need real flexibility in your schedule. If you're on a tight itinerary, skip it. If you have a full day to spare and you don't mind getting back late, go.

The road past Sissu is generally good, but it narrows in places. Watch for loose gravel and the occasional landslide debris. The track up to the fort is uneven. Wear shoes with good grip. There's no shade on the climb, so carry water and a hat.

No facilities at the fort. No shop, no toilet, no bench. The village below has a small shop for water and biscuits, but don't count on it being open. Pack what you need.

Best time of year is late spring through early autumn. The fort is at around 8,000 feet elevation. Snow blocks the track in winter, and the tunnel itself closes occasionally for maintenance. [Check live Rohtang Pass status](https://manali.today/is-rohtang-pass-open-today) as a proxy for weather conditions on that side of the tunnel, though the pass and the tunnel are different routes. You can also check the [India Meteorological Department forecast for Lahaul and Spiti](https://mausam.imd.gov.in/) for more detailed weather planning.

Fitness level needed is moderate. The climb is short but steep. Anyone who can walk uphill for twenty minutes without stopping will manage fine. If you have mobility issues, the fort is probably not accessible.

## Who Should Skip This

If your idea of a good day in Manali is visiting three cafes and buying a pashmina, skip Gondhla. There's nothing to buy. No Instagram setup. No place to sit and order a latte.

If you're traveling with small children who can't handle uneven terrain, skip it. The drop-offs near the fort walls are real, and there are no railings.

If you're on a strict schedule and you need to be back in Manali by evening, skip it. The traffic through Atal Tunnel is unpredictable. Adding two hours for the Gondhla detour could push you into a late return, and driving that road in the dark is not something I recommend.

But if you have the time, if you're tired of crowds, if you want to stand somewhere that feels genuinely old and genuinely forgotten, Gondhla is worth the extra kilometers. Most people cross Atal Tunnel, reach Sissu, take a U-turn, and head straight back. You don't have to be most people.
