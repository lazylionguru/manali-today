Every spring I get the same question from people planning their Manali trip. "When does Rohtang Pass open?" They want a date they can circle on a calendar. They want certainty.

I've been watching this pass open and close since I moved here in 2018. I run the live Rohtang status checker on manali.today myself. And the honest answer is: nobody can give you that date more than a few weeks out, because the pass opens when the snow lets it open, not when the tourism calendar says it should.

Let me explain what's actually happening up there, and what that means for your trip.

## What's Actually Happening

Rohtang Pass sits at around 3,980 meters. That's nearly double Manali's own elevation of 2,050 meters. Every winter, that stretch of road gets buried under snow that can be several meters deep. The [Border Roads Organisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Border_Roads_Organisation), or BRO, has the job of clearing it.

Here's what most travel blogs won't tell you. The BRO doesn't start with a fixed opening date and work backward. They start with the snow and work forward. They clear from both sides, pushing through drifts that can be as tall as a two-story building. The final stretch, the snowbound section near the top, is what determines when the pass actually opens.

That final stretch is unpredictable. Some years it melts fast. Some years it holds on. The opening date shifts year to year because the weather decides, not a calendar.

## What This Means for Your Trip

If you're planning a trip around Rohtang, here's the reality.

**The downsides first.**

The biggest problem is uncertainty. You might book your trip for mid-May hoping the pass will be open, only to arrive and find the BRO still working on the final stretch. You might plan for early June and hit the opening day crowds. Everyone wants to be first across. The traffic on opening day and the following week can be brutal. Cars lined up at the checkpoint, waiting for clearance, sitting in queues that stretch for kilometers.

There's also the road condition itself. Even after the pass opens, the road isn't a smooth highway. It's a mountain pass that's been buried under snow for months. There can be loose gravel, narrow sections, and spots where the road is still wet or icy. Not dangerous if you're careful, but not a casual Sunday drive either.

**The upsides.**

When you catch it right, the payoff is real. The views from Rohtang in late spring are something else. Fresh snow on the peaks, the valley greening up below, the air clean and cold. You can stand at the pass and see both the Kullu valley behind you and the Lahaul valley ahead. It's worth the wait.

And if you're heading further into Lahaul and Spiti, Rohtang opening is your gateway. The road to Keylong, to the monasteries, to the high altitude villages. That's the real reason people wait for this pass. It's not just a tourist stop. It's a connection to an entire region.

## What I'd Actually Tell You to Do

Don't plan your trip around a specific opening date. Plan your trip around the window when the pass *might* open, and build flexibility into your schedule.

If you're coming in the first half of May, assume the pass will be closed. Plan your Manali time around the valley itself. There's plenty to do here without going over Rohtang. If it opens early, that's a bonus. If it doesn't, you're not disappointed.

If you're coming in the second half of May or early June, you have better odds. But still, give yourself a few extra days. If the pass opens while you're here, great. If it's delayed by a week, you have time to wait.

If you're coming after mid-June, the pass is almost certainly open. But you'll be dealing with crowds and traffic. The [checkpoint system](https://rohtangpermits.hp.gov.in) is there for a reason. It limits the number of vehicles going up each day. Book your permit early and plan for an early morning departure.
