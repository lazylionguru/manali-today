I get this question almost every day now. Someone messages me on Instagram or stops me near the Mall Road and says, "Narender, we're driving through Atal Tunnel next week. What do we need to know?"

The short answer is simple. But the useful answer takes a few more words. So here it is, written the way I'd tell you if we were sitting at a dhaba in Old Manali.

## What You Actually Need to Know

Let me clear up the biggest confusion first.

**No permit required.** None. Zero. Unlike [Rohtang Pass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rohtang_Pass), where you need to apply online days in advance and hope the quota isn't full, Atal Tunnel has no such system. Every vehicle can enter. Private cars, rented Scorpios, even commercial vehicles. You just drive in.

This surprises a lot of people. They show up thinking they need paperwork. They don't.

**The tunnel is open 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.** In normal conditions, you can drive through at 3 AM if you want. The actual drive takes under 15 minutes. The tunnel is about 9 kilometers long. That's it. You enter one side, you come out the other, and the world changes completely.

**But normal conditions don't always last.** Closures happen. Weather advisories, avalanche risk, scheduled maintenance, or emergencies can shut it temporarily. I've seen people plan their entire day around driving through, only to reach the south portal and find barriers down. Don't assume it's always open. Before you head out, [check live Atal Tunnel status](https://manali.today/is-atal-tunnel-open-today) on manali.today. I update it myself based on what I hear from my contacts at the tunnel authority and what I see on the ground.

## What to Expect Inside and at Both Portals

The tunnel itself is straightforward. Well lit, smooth road, proper ventilation. You won't feel claustrophobic unless you're prone to that sort of thing. There are no stops allowed inside. No pulling over for photos, no sudden braking because you spotted something interesting. Keep moving.

**The south portal is on the Manali side.** This is the Kullu valley. Lush green, pine trees, river flowing nearby, everything you associate with Manali. There are cafes and dhabas right near the entrance. Good chai, parathas, the usual. You can stop here before going in or after coming back out.

**The north portal is on the Sissu side.** This is Lahaul. Dry, stark, brown mountains, completely different vegetation. The contrast is part of why people make the trip. You exit the tunnel and the landscape shifts entirely. It feels like you've driven into another country, because in a way you have. Sissu itself has a few cafes and dhabas near the portal. Nothing fancy, but enough for a break.

Most tourists drive through, spend an hour or two on the Lahaul side taking photos near the Sissu waterfall or the lake, and drive back. That's a perfectly good way to do it.

**A note on timing.** Since the tunnel is open 24 hours, you can go early morning to avoid crowds. Weekends get busy. Weekday mornings are quiet. If you want the portals to yourself, aim for 7 AM on a Tuesday.

## What Can Go Wrong

I don't say this to scare you. I say it because I've watched too many tourists make the same mistakes.

**Closures.** The tunnel closes without much notice sometimes. Heavy snowfall on the Lahaul side, avalanche warnings, maintenance work. If you've driven all the way from Delhi and planned your entire trip around driving through this tunnel, a closure can ruin your day. That's why I keep the live status page updated. [check live Rohtang Pass status](https://manali.today/is-rohtang-pass-open-today) too, because sometimes people confuse the two or assume one being open means the other is.

**Weather on the Lahaul side.** Even when the tunnel is open, the weather on the north side can be bad. Snow, fog, zero visibility. You exit the tunnel and suddenly you're in a whiteout. Drive carefully. Carry chains if there's snow forecast. The tunnel itself is fine, but the road beyond it is mountain road, and mountain road demands respect. Check the [India Meteorological Department forecast](https://mausam.imd.gov.in) for the region before you go.

**No fuel stations.** This is the one that gets people. There are no petrol pumps right around the tunnel or in Sissu. None. Start with a full tank if you're heading toward Lahaul or beyond. I've seen people run low and panic. The nearest fuel is back in Manali or further ahead in Keylong. Don't gamble on it.

**Altitude.** The tunnel sits at about 3,000 meters. The Lahaul side is higher. If you're coming from sea level and driving straight through, you might feel the altitude. Headache, dizziness, shortness of breath. Take it slow. Spend time acclimatizing in Manali first. Don't drive through and immediately start hiking.

## Narender's Honest Take

Atal Tunnel is worth doing. It changed how people travel to Lahaul and Spiti. What used to be a six hour drive over Rohtang Pass, weather permitting, is now a fifteen minute drive through a tunnel, almost always open.

But it's not a destination in itself. It's a corridor. The point is what lies beyond. The Chandra valley, Keylong, the monasteries, the high passes further north. If you drive through, take a selfie at the north portal sign, and drive back, you've seen the tunnel but you haven't seen why the tunnel matters.

If you have time, go further. Spend a night in Sissu or Tandi or Keylong. The landscape changes in ways Manali doesn't prepare you for.
