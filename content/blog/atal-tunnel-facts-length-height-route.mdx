Every week someone messages me the same questions. How long is the tunnel? Do I need a permit? Can I stop inside for photos? The confusion is understandable. Before the tunnel opened, [Rohtang Pass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rohtang_Pass) was the only route to Lahaul and Spiti, and that required permits, had limited hours, and closed for half the year. People still carry that mindset.

So here is everything I actually get asked, answered directly.

## How Long Is Atal Tunnel?

The tunnel is 9.02 kilometers long. That is the number everyone wants first, so here it is. It runs under the Rohtang Pass in Himachal Pradesh, connecting the Kullu valley on the Manali side with Lahaul valley on the other side.

The south portal is a few kilometers from Solang. The north portal is a few kilometers from Sissu. Between them, 9.02 km of road carved through solid rock. For more technical details, you can read the [Atal Tunnel entry on Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atal_Tunnel).

## Do I Need a Permit for Atal Tunnel?

No. That is the simplest answer I can give.

Unlike Rohtang Pass, which still requires permits for private vehicles beyond certain points, Atal Tunnel is open to all vehicles year round. No permit. No paperwork. No booking window. You drive up, you go through.

This catches a lot of people off guard. They show up at the tunnel entrance looking for a permit counter. There isn't one. Just drive.

If you are heading toward Rohtang Pass itself rather than the tunnel, that is a different story. You can [check live Rohtang Pass status](https://manali.today/is-rohtang-pass-open-today) to see whether the pass is open and what restrictions apply on any given day.

## Speed Limits and Cameras

Inside the tunnel, the speed limit changes depending on where you are. For the first few kilometers at both the entry and exit, the limit is 40 kmph. In the middle section, it goes up to 60 kmph.

Cameras are everywhere inside. They are not hidden. They are mounted on the walls and ceiling at regular intervals. If you speed, you will get caught. The system is automated and it works.

A lot of drivers treat the tunnel like a straight highway and push past 80 or 90 kmph. That is how accidents happen.

## The North Portal Exit Turn

This is the detail I mention to every friend who asks about driving the tunnel. As you exit the north portal, there is a sharp turn immediately to the right.

A car went into the river there recently because the driver came out of the tunnel too fast and could not make the turn. The tunnel exit deposits you onto a road that curves hard right, and if you are still doing 60 or 70 kmph when you come out, you will not have enough road to correct.

Drive within the regulated speed as you approach the exit. That turn is not a joke.

## No Stopping, No Overtaking, No Sunroofs

Three rules inside the tunnel, and they are all enforced.

No stopping. If your vehicle breaks down, pull as far to the side as possible and use the emergency phones. But do not stop for photos, for a quick look around, or to let someone else catch up. Keep moving.

No overtaking. The tunnel has two lanes, one in each direction. Stay in your lane. The lanes are narrow enough that overtaking puts you and oncoming traffic in a bad spot.

No sticking your head out of a sunroof. This sounds like something nobody would do, but people do it. They want a video of the tunnel lights streaming past from above the car. It is extremely dangerous and also illegal. The tunnel has overhead clearance that does not account for a person standing through a sunroof. One bump, one maintenance fixture, and the result is serious injury or worse.

Honking is discouraged unless necessary. The tunnel amplifies sound. A horn blast inside 9 km of concrete is loud and disorienting for other drivers.

## Mobile Network Works Inside

This surprises people. You get full mobile network coverage throughout the 9.02 km. Calls, messages, data, all of it works.

That is useful if you need to contact someone on the other side, check road conditions ahead, or just let your hotel know your ETA. But please do not use it to livestream yourself driving through the tunnel. Keep your attention on the road.

## Year Round Access

The tunnel is open all year. That is the whole point of building it. Before the tunnel, Rohtang Pass closed for six to seven months every winter, cutting off Lahaul and Spiti from the rest of the state. Now you can drive through regardless of snow on the pass above.

That does not mean the roads on either side of the tunnel are always clear. Snow can still accumulate at the south portal near Solang and at the north portal near Sissu. But the tunnel itself stays operational.

For live updates on whether snow is affecting access on either side, you can [check live Atal Tunnel status](https://manali.today/is-atal-tunnel-open-today). I update that page based on what I see and hear from contacts on both ends.

## What About Rohtang Pass?

People often confuse the tunnel with the pass. They are different routes to the same region.

The tunnel goes under the pass. It is faster, safer, and open year round. The pass goes over the top and requires permits, has limited vehicle numbers per day, and closes in winter.

If you want to go to Lahaul and Spiti, use the tunnel. If you specifically want to go to the Rohtang Pass snow point for a few hours, you need a permit and you need to check conditions first. I keep a [check live Rohtang Pass status](https://manali.today/is-rohtang-pass-open-today) page for exactly that reason.

## A Few Other Things Worth Knowing

The tunnel is 9.02 km long but the drive feels shorter because the lighting is consistent and the road is smooth. There are emergency exits at intervals along the length, marked clearly. If you ever need to evacuate, follow those signs.

The temperature inside the tunnel stays cooler than the outside air, especially in summer. That feels good for the first minute, but after a few kilometers you might want to turn the heater on if you are on a motorcycle. The cold seeps in.

Motorcycles are allowed through. No special restrictions. Just follow the same rules about speed, no overtaking, and no stopping.

## If You Want to Explore Beyond the Tunnel

Once you exit at the north portal, you are in Lahaul valley. Sissu is the first village, and it has a waterfall and some basic eateries. Further on is Keylong, then Jispa, then the high passes toward Leh.

But if you are staying on the Manali side and want quieter places, there are spots worth seeking out. Sethan village, up above Manali, is a quiet escape with its own mythology about the Pandavas hiding there. I wrote about it in my post on [Sethan: The Village Where the Pandavas Reportedly Hid, Now a Quiet Escape](https://manali.today/blog/sethan-village-offbeat-manali).

There is also a trail through Bhanara and past the Nag Devta temple that leads to Baghi Meadow, a clearing most tourists never see. That route is in my post about [The Hidden Trail to Baghi Meadow Through Bhanara and Nag Devta Temple](https://manali.today/blog/bhanara-nag-devta-baghi-hidden-trail).

And if you are driving around Manali during peak summer, be prepared for traffic that does not show up in the glossy Instagram photos. I wrote about that in [Why Manali Traffic Photos Online Are Missing the Point](https://manali.today/blog/manali-peak-summer-traffic-survival-guide).

## Before You Go

Check the snow conditions before you head toward the tunnel, especially in winter and early spring. The roads on both sides can get slippery. I update [check live snow status](https://manali.today/) regularly so you know what to expect.

The tunnel itself is straightforward. 9.02 km. No permit. No stopping. Watch your speed at the north portal exit. That is really all you need to know.

Drive safe.
