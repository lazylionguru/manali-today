I get asked about this hike a lot. Friends message me saying they saw some Reel about a cliff in Manali, a big rock hanging off the hillside, and they want to know how to find it. The name scares people off. Suicide Point. It sounds like something grim, some dark tourist spot. But the name undersells the place completely. It's a striking large cracked rock hanging off the hillside, and the walk to get there is one of the most straightforward and rewarding short hikes you can do straight out of Old Manali.

Let me walk you through it, start to finish, the way I'd tell a friend who just showed up at my door.

## The Trail, Start to Finish

You start from the alley near the temple parking by Rama Dhaba in Old Manali. If you know the area, you know the spot. If you don't, just head toward the main temple in Old Manali, find the parking area near Rama Dhaba, and look for the alley. It's not hard to miss. Locals use this route regularly.

The alley turns into a trail heading down toward the river. This first section is the steepest part of the whole walk. You drop down quickly, and the sound of the river gets louder as you go. The trail is well worn, people have been walking this path for years. You'll pass through some trees, mostly [deodar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cedrus_deodara) and pine, and the air changes once you're off the main road. It cools down, smells like earth and pine needles.

After about ten minutes of descending, you reach a small water station. It's a good spot to sit and cool off. There's a pipe with running water, mountain water, cold and clean. I always stop here. Not because I need the rest, but because it's a peaceful place. You can hear the river clearly, the water from the pipe splashing onto the rocks. Sit on the flat stones nearby. Take a few minutes. Most people rush past this spot on their way to the point, but I think it's half the reason to do the walk.

Now here is where most people make a mistake. They see the river, they see the water, and they think the trail ends there. They turn around. Or they scramble down to the riverbank, which is fine if that's what you want. But the real hike, the one that takes you to Suicide Point, keeps going up instead.

From the water station, you cross a small wooden bridge or find your way across the stream depending on the season. The trail continues upward from there. It climbs steadily, switchbacking through the forest. This section is steeper than the initial descent, but it's not punishing. The trail is clear, no scrambling required, just a good steady incline through the trees.

The forest opens up as you gain elevation. You start to see glimpses of the valley below, the river snaking through, the rooftops of Old Manali on the other side. Keep going. The trail levels out slightly, and then you see it.

Suicide Point. A large cracked rock hanging off the hillside. It looks like it could fall at any moment, but it's been there for centuries. The crack runs through the middle of it, wide enough to see through in places. You can walk out onto the rock, carefully, and stand at the edge. The drop below is sheer. The valley spreads out in front of you, the river a thin silver line far below.

People sit on that rock for a long time. They take photos, sure, but mostly they just sit and look. There's no railing, no safety barrier, just the rock and the drop. You have to be careful. But that's also what makes it real.

## What Makes It Worth Doing

I've done this hike maybe thirty times since 2018. I still do it. Not every week, but often enough that the trail knows my boots.

What makes it worth doing is the simplicity. It's not a full day expedition. You don't need a guide. You don't need special gear. You walk out of Old Manali, down an alley, and within an hour you're standing on a rock that feels like it's floating above the valley. That's rare. Most hikes in this region require a drive first, then a long walk. This one starts from your guesthouse.

The water station is genuinely a good spot. I don't say that about many places. Most "scenic spots" on trails are overrated, just places where someone put a bench. But this one is different. The water is cold and drinkable. The sound of the river fills the whole space. You can sit there for twenty minutes and not see another person, depending on the time of day.

And the rock itself. That cracked rock. It's not a viewpoint with a sign and a ticket booth. It's a real geological feature, a piece of the mountain that split apart at some point in the distant past and stayed. The crack is deep enough that you can put your hand inside it, feel the cold stone. The view from the edge is unobstructed. You see the whole [Kharma Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kullu_district), the way the hills fold into each other, the river cutting through.

If you have read my post on [Old Manali: Where I Actually Live, and How I'd Spend a Day There](https://manali.today/blog/old-manali-guide-cafes-hikes), you know I don't recommend things lightly. This hike makes the list.

## Practical Notes

Best time of day is morning. Start by 8 AM, earlier in peak summer. The trail is shaded for most of the walk, but the rock itself gets direct sun by late morning. Also, the light is better for seeing the valley clearly in the morning. Afternoon haze can blur the view.

Fitness level needed is moderate. The total walk is maybe 3 to 4 kilometers round trip. The climb from the water station to the point is the hardest part, but it's short, maybe fifteen to twenty minutes of steady uphill. If you can walk up a few flights of stairs without getting winded, you can do this hike.

Footwear matters. I see people doing this in flip-flops. Don't. The trail is dusty in dry season, muddy in wet season. The rock itself can be slippery, especially if there's morning dew. Wear shoes with grip. Hiking sandals work fine, just something that stays on your feet.

Wildlife. I have seen monkeys on this trail, and once a barking deer near the water station. The monkeys can be aggressive if you have food visible. Keep snacks in your bag. Don't feed them.

The name Suicide Point bothers some people. I understand. But that's what it's called locally, and it's not because people jump. It's an old name, probably from the height and the sheer drop. The locals call it that without drama. It's just a name.

Before you go, [check live Rohtang Pass status](https://manali.today/is-rohtang-pass-open-today) and [check live Atal Tunnel status](https://manali.today/is-atal-tunnel-open-today) if you're planning to combine this hike with a drive up to the pass. The tunnel and pass conditions affect how crowded the valley gets. On days when Rohtang is closed, this trail is quieter.

## Who Should Skip This

If you have a serious fear of heights, skip the rock itself. You can still do the walk to the water station and back, that's a lovely hike on its own. But standing on that cracked rock with a drop on three sides is not for everyone. I have seen people freeze up once they reach the edge. No shame in that.

If you are traveling with very young children, this might not be the best choice. The trail is not dangerous, but there are sections where a child could slip. And the rock at the top has no barrier. I see parents bring kids up there and hold their hands tight the whole time. It's doable, but stressful.

If you only have one day in Manali and you want to see the big sights, do something else. [Solang Valley](https://himachaltourism.gov.in/destination/solang-valley/), the temples, the mall road. This hike is for people who want a quiet morning, a real walk, something that feels like it belongs to the place rather than to Instagram.
