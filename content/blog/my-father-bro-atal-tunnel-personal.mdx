## What People Don't See When They Drive Through in Fifteen Minutes

You cross it in fifteen minutes. Maybe less if traffic is light. The tunnel is well lit, the road is smooth, the walls are clean. You come out the other side into the Lahaul valley and you keep going toward Leh or back toward Manali and you don't think about it again.

That's fine. That's how it should work. A road's job is to be invisible when it's working.

But I sit here in Old Manali watching the live feeds on manali.today and I watch people drive through Atal Tunnel every day in summer. Thousands of cars. Most people don't know what that road cost. I don't mean the money. I mean the years, the method, the lives.

I have reasons to think about it more than most. But even without those reasons, the tunnel deserves a second thought from anyone who uses it.

## A Personal Note

My father served with the Border Roads Organisation. I keep the specifics of his service general out of respect for his privacy, but I grew up around that world. BRO builds roads and bridges in places where roads and bridges should not exist. My father was part of that work.

So when I see Atal Tunnel, I see a project that people in his line of work spent decades dreaming about. The Rohtang Pass was always the bottleneck. Every year the snow closed it for six months. Every year the army, the locals, the truck drivers, the tourists all waited. A tunnel under the pass was talked about since the 1960s. It took until 2020 to open.

I check the live Atal Tunnel status every day on manali.today because I know what it means when that road is open versus when it is closed. But I also check it because I know what it took to build.

## What BRO Actually Does

Most people hear "Border Roads Organisation" and think of military logistics. That is part of it. BRO is a tri-service organisation under India's Ministry of Defence. They specialise in road and bridge construction in difficult terrain. They have built over 50,000 kilometres of roads and more than 450 permanent bridges across India's border regions. You can read more about their history and mandate on [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Border_Roads_Organisation).

But that is the headline version.

The work nobody sees is the snow clearance at three in the morning. The disaster response when a landslide cuts off a village. The constant maintenance of roads that get destroyed every monsoon and every winter. BRO does not just build things and leave. They stay and keep the roads open.

That is the part I grew up around. My father did not talk much about the big projects. He talked about the work. The day to day. The crews that go out in weather where nobody should be on a mountain.

Atal Tunnel is the famous project. But it is one project among thousands. BRO has been doing this work for decades. They will be doing it for decades more.

## What a Project Like This Actually Costs

Atal Tunnel was not built with a tunnel boring machine. That is the first thing people assume when they hear about a tunnel. A big machine drills through the mountain and the tunnel appears behind it. Simple.

This tunnel was built using the drill and blast technique with the New Austrian Tunnelling Method. That means explosives. That means hand drilling. That means constant monitoring of the rock because the ground here does not behave. The engineers did not use a tunnel boring machine because TBMs risked jamming in the high deformation and pressure conditions of this terrain. The mountain moves. The rock shifts. A machine gets stuck and you cannot get it out. The [New Austrian Tunnelling Method](https://www.britannica.com/technology/New-Austrian-Tunnelling-method) is a well-documented approach for such challenging geology.

So men with drills and explosives did it the hard way.

The excavation had to happen mostly from the south portal alone for years. The north portal was snowed in and inaccessible for more than half the year. So for long stretches, all the work happened from the Manali side. The crews worked toward the other end knowing they would not meet it for years.

And then there is the cost that does not get mentioned in the tourism brochures.

On 8 August 2003, a cloudburst and flash flood hit the site. Forty two labourers died. They were building the temporary access road. Not the tunnel itself. The road to get to the tunnel. That is the kind of detail that gets lost. Forty two people died building a road so that other people could build a tunnel so that tourists could save fifteen minutes driving over a pass.

I do not say this to be dramatic. I say it because it is true. Every road in these mountains has a cost like that. Some are known. Most are not.

The tunnel took ten years. Ten years of drilling and blasting and monitoring and waiting for the two sides to meet. Ten years of families waiting for their men to come home from the mountain.
