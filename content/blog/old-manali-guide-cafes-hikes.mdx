I moved to Old Manali in 2018. Before that, I was like most visitors. I came for a week, stayed near Mall Road, ate at the same crowded restaurants everyone eats at, and left thinking I'd seen Manali. I hadn't.

Seven years later, I run manali.today from a table at Krishna Dhaba. I watch the snow reports come in, I talk to drivers coming down from Rohtang, and I live in a part of town that most tourists pass through but never really stop in. This is the Old Manali guide I wish someone had handed me back then.

## What Old Manali Actually Is

Old Manali sits on the other side of the Manalsu River, a ten minute walk from the main bridge. It's busier than Shanag or Burwa, two villages further up the valley that are genuinely quiet. But compared to Mall Road, Old Manali feels like a different country.

The main lane is narrow. Cars squeeze past each other. Guesthouses stack up the hillside. What makes Old Manali work is the cafe culture. Small places, family run, with real tables and real people sitting at them. The river walk runs along the bank below the main road. You can hear the water from almost anywhere.

The people who stay here tend to stay longer. Not just tourists. Writers, photographers, people working remotely, people like me who started with a week and never quite left. The long term community gathers in the same few spots daily. Krishna Dhaba is one of them. Small family run place, honest prices, a genuinely lovely owner who remembers your name and your order. I do some of my manali.today work from there. The chai is good and nobody rushes you.

## Why I'd Send You Here (or Not)

If you want to be in the middle of everything, stay on Mall Road. If you want absolute silence, go to Shanag or Burga. Old Manali sits in the middle. You get the cafe culture, you get the river, you get trails leaving from your doorstep. But you also get noise from the main lane during peak season and some guesthouses packed close together.

I'd send you here if you are the kind of person who wants to wake up, walk five minutes to a trail, spend the morning hiking, then sit at a dhaba for two hours reading a book. If you want nightclubs and shopping rows, this is not your place.

The complete Manali travel guide on manali.today covers all the neighbourhoods in detail. Old Manali is where I live. It is where I would send a friend who said "I want to actually experience Manali, not just see it."

## What to Actually Do There

I get asked this a lot. People want a list. I'll give you three things, because three good things beat ten mediocre ones.

### The Kharma Valley Trail

Walk up the main lane past the temple. Near the temple parking, there is an alley beside Rama Dhaba. Take it. The trail drops down toward the river, passes the water station, then climbs up to Suicide Point.

The name is worse than the place. Suicide Point is a large cracked rock with a wide view of the valley. The crack runs straight through the middle of the rock. Locals say it formed during an earthquake decades ago. The hike takes about an hour each way at a steady pace. You will pass apple orchards and small streams. Take water. There is no shop at the top.

### The Three Meadows from Rocky's Cafe

Rocky's Cafe is on the far end of the main lane, past most of the guesthouses. A trail starts behind it and heads up through three meadows. One after another, each higher and quieter than the last.

The first meadow has a few cows and a small shed. The second meadow opens up wider, with views of the surrounding peaks. The third meadow is where the trail ends. You can sit there for an hour and see maybe one other person. I have checked live snow status from that meadow more times than I can count, because the cell signal gets spotty and I want to make sure the manali.today feed is updating.

### The Goshal Village Trail

From Rocky's Cafe, instead of going left toward the meadows, take the right hand trail. This one leads toward Goshal village. It is a longer walk, about two and a half hours round trip if you go all the way. But there are good stopping points along the way.

About thirty minutes in, there is a flat rock next to a small waterfall. About an hour in, you hit a clearing where you can see the entire Old Manali valley spread below. The trail ends at Goshal itself, a small village with a few houses and a tiny temple. The people there are used to hikers passing through. They will nod and go back to their work.

## Practical Notes

The trails I mentioned are safe in clear weather. Do not attempt them in heavy rain or after dark. The Kharma Valley trail has loose sections near the river. Watch your footing.

Old Manali is a ten minute walk from the main bridge that connects to Mall Road. You do not need a vehicle if you are staying here. Everything is walkable.

For road conditions coming into Manali itself, I update the check live Atal Tunnel status page on manali.today daily. The tunnel changes things. It used to take four hours to reach Manali from the other side. Now it takes one. But the tunnel closes for maintenance and weather. Check before you drive.

I also keep a browse the Manali travel guide page with general advice on where to stay, what to pack, and how to handle the altitude. Old Manali sits at about 2,000 meters. You will feel it on the hikes if you arrive from sea level. Take the first day easy. For more on the region's geography and history, you can read about [Manali on Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/place/Manali). The [Himachal Pradesh Tourism website](https://himachaltourism.gov.in) also has official information on permits and attractions.

Come see it for yourself. And if you get lost, ask at Krishna Dhaba. Someone there will point you the right way.
