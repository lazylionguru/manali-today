## It's Not Just About Snow

Every summer, I get messages from people who planned their entire Manali trip around Rohtang Pass. They check the weather, see clear skies, and assume they're good. Then Tuesday rolls around and they're stuck at the checkpoint wondering what happened.

Rohtang Pass closes every Tuesday. Not because of snow. Not because of bad weather. Just because it's Tuesday.

This is a standing weekly closure for routine maintenance. The [Border Roads Organisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Border_Roads_Organisation) uses that day for road repairs, avalanche barrier checks, and general upkeep. It happens regardless of conditions. I've seen crystal clear Tuesday mornings where the pass sits there looking perfectly accessible, and it's still shut. Tourists pull up, argue with the checkpoint staff, and waste half their day learning a lesson they could have learned from a two minute read.

The Tuesday closure is separate from any weather related closure. That means even if you have a permit, even if the forecast is perfect, you cannot cross Rohtang on a Tuesday. Plan around it.

## The Permit Is a Separate Thing Entirely

A lot of people confuse "do I have a permit" with "is the pass open." These are two completely different questions.

The permit is a paperwork requirement from the Himachal Pradesh government. You get it through their portal at rohtangpermits.hp.gov.in. It controls how many vehicles go up each day, meant to manage traffic and environmental impact. You need one regardless of whether the pass is open or closed on any given day.

But having a permit does not mean the pass is open. I've seen people wave their permit at the checkpoint like it's a magic key. It's not. If the pass is closed for maintenance, weather, or any other reason, your permit is just a piece of digital paper. The permit system and the closure system run on separate logic. Don't conflate them.

## A Little History Behind the Caution

There's a reason people take Rohtang seriously. The name itself carries weight. In local tradition, "Rohtang" is often said to mean something close to "pile of corpses." That's not a tourism marketing line. It reflects what this pass used to be before modern road maintenance.

Old timers in Manali still tell stories of sudden blizzards that would trap travelers for days. Before the road was properly engineered, the pass could turn deadly fast. A clear morning could become a whiteout by afternoon. People died up there. Enough of them that the name stuck.

I'm not saying this for shock value. I'm saying it because the caution around Rohtang is earned. When BRO closes the pass for avalanche risk, they're not being bureaucratic. They're responding to conditions that have killed people historically. The modern road is far safer, but the mountain hasn't changed. Snowpack, wind, temperature gradients, these things still behave the same way they did a hundred years ago.

So when you see a closure notice and think "it looks fine from here," remember that the people making that call have data you don't. They know the history.

## How Atal Tunnel Changed the Calculus

Before [Atal Tunnel](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Atal-Tunnel) existed, Rohtang Pass was the only route to Lahaul and Spiti. That meant BRO had to keep it open as long as humanly possible. They would clear snow around the clock during spring, push the season as far as they could, and accept the closure only when it became physically impossible. The pass used to close for roughly six months every winter. That was just how life worked.

Atal Tunnel changed everything.

Now, vehicles can reach Lahaul year round through the tunnel, regardless of what's happening on top of the pass. That's a massive improvement for the people who actually live in Lahaul, who no longer get cut off for half the year. But it also changed how BRO prioritizes Rohtang itself.

Since the tunnel handles through traffic, BRO no longer needs to keep Rohtang Pass clear for vehicles heading to Lahaul. The urgency is gone. Snow clearance on the pass now happens on a different schedule. Maintenance gets done when it gets done. If conditions are marginal, the decision leans toward closure rather than pushing through. The risk calculus shifted.

This is why you'll sometimes see Rohtang closed even when the snow doesn't look that bad. The old imperative to keep it open at all costs is gone. The pass is now managed more like a scenic high altitude road and less like a critical transport corridor. That's fine for the people of Lahaul. It's frustrating for tourists who came specifically to drive over the pass.
