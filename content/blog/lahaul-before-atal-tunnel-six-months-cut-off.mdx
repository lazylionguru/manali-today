I moved to Old Manali in 2018, a year before the Atal Tunnel broke through the mountain. Even then, the old stories were still fresh in everyone's mouth. People in Lahaul had been living a different kind of life, one that tourists driving through in forty minutes today will never fully understand.

I'm not going to romanticize it. The isolation was brutal. But it also shaped a community in ways that matter, even now.

## Six Months, Cut Off

Rohtang Pass sits at 13,050 feet. Before the tunnel, that pass was the only road connecting Lahaul valley to the rest of Himachal Pradesh. When winter came and the snow piled up, that road disappeared. For roughly six to eight months every year, the valley was completely cut off.

Not "hard to reach." Not "inconvenient." Cut off. No trucks. No buses. No ambulances. No way in and no way out.

The snow didn't care about your plans. It didn't care if you had a medical emergency, a wedding to attend, or a child who needed to get to school. Rohtang closed when it closed, and that was that.

## What That Actually Meant, Day to Day

Let me give you the specifics, because the abstraction doesn't do it justice.

Every household in Lahaul had to stock six months of rations, fuel, and fodder by October. That meant rice, wheat, cooking oil, lentils, kerosene, medicine, and enough dry grass and hay to keep the livestock alive through the winter. If you forgot something, if you miscalculated, if your supplies ran short in February, there was no running to the shop. There was no shop. There was nothing.

Over 60 percent of Lahaul's roughly 18,000 residents used to migrate to Kullu every winter before the tunnel. Think about that. More than half the population packed up their lives and left their homes for half the year. They rented rooms in Manali, in Bhuntar, in Kullu town. Children changed schools. Families split up. Only the ones who had to stay behind, the ones with livestock that couldn't travel or elderly family members who couldn't make the journey, stayed in the valley through the dark months.

The medical emergencies were the worst part. Before helicopters became more reliable, if someone got seriously sick in December, the village had to carry them. Across Rohtang Pass. At over 13,000 feet. In snow that could swallow a man up to his chest. Villagers would take turns, strapping the sick person to their backs and walking through whiteout conditions, hoping they made it to Manali before it was too late.

Even after helicopters became available, the weather didn't always cooperate. There are stories in every village, people who died waiting for a helicopter that couldn't fly because the clouds wouldn't lift. The families still live there. They remember.

I've heard these stories from old men in Keylong who tell them the way you'd tell someone about a bad winter, not as tragedy but as fact. This is what life was. You dealt with it or you left.

## What Still Happens Today, Even With the Tunnel

The tunnel changed everything for most of Lahaul. You can drive from Manali to Keylong in about two hours now, any day of the year. The migration numbers have dropped dramatically. Medical evacuations are a phone call and a drive, not a prayer.

But I don't want to pretend the tunnel solved every problem.

Some villages near Koksar, just a few kilometers from the tunnel's north portal, still experience heavy enough snowfall that residents migrate seasonally. The tunnel gets you through the mountain, but it doesn't stop the snow from falling on the valley floor. If you live in a village that gets buried under ten feet of snow every winter, you're still walking on rooftops to get around. You're still shoveling your door open every morning. Some families still choose to spend the worst months in Kullu.

The difference is that now they can come back whenever they want. The tunnel means they're not trapped. They can leave in November and drive back for a wedding in January if they need to. That freedom, the ability to choose, is something the previous generation never had.

## Why This Context Matters

I see tourists drive through the tunnel every day. They take a photo at the north portal, maybe stop for tea in Sissu, and drive back to Manali for dinner. They have no idea what that road used to cost.

Every time someone asks me what they should see in Lahaul, I tell them to slow down. The tunnel is an engineering marvel, sure, but what matters more is what it means for the people who live there. A child in Keylong can now get to a hospital in Manali in two hours instead of dying on a stretcher carried across a frozen pass. A family can stay in their home through the winter instead of packing up and leaving for six months. A farmer can sell his potatoes in Manali in January instead of watching them rot in a cold storage room.

That's not a tourist story. That's a human one.
