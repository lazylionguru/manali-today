People ask me about Solang Valley paragliding almost every day. Someone from Delhi messages me on the site, someone else stops me near the Mall Road and says "Narender, is it worth it?" I've been watching this valley since 2018, and I have a straight answer for you.

Let me walk you through it.

## The Activity, Start to Finish

Solang Valley sits at around 2,560m. That's about 13 to 14 km from Manali town. You drive north on the main road, past the last few hotels, and the valley opens up on your left. It's a wide U-shaped glacial valley with a river running through the bottom and pine forests climbing the sides.

For paragliding, you don't launch from the valley floor. You drive up the mountain road to the takeoff site. The main launch points sit between 2,400m and 2,600m, depending on which operator you book with. Different companies have different agreements with landowners up there, so the exact height varies by a hundred meters or so. It doesn't change the experience much.

Here's how a typical morning goes.

You arrive at the Solang parking area. Your operator meets you there, takes you to a small counter or tent where you sign a waiver. Then you climb into a shared jeep or a Tata Sumo for the bumpy ride up to the launch site. That road is unpaved, narrow in places, and the driver knows every rock on it. The ride takes about 15 to 20 minutes.

At the top, you wait. Not in a bad way. The pilots watch the wind, check their instruments, talk to each other on the radio. They're looking for consistent thermal activity and steady wind direction. If the wind is gusty or coming from the wrong angle, nobody takes off. That's a good sign, actually. It means they care about safety.

When the pilot says it's time, you stand at the edge of the slope. They hook you into the harness. The canopy goes up behind you. The pilot gives the command. You run. The ground drops away.

The flight itself lasts somewhere between 8 and 15 minutes, depending on conditions. You're not alone up there. You share the air with maybe a dozen other canopies, all drifting in the same thermal. The pilot handles the steering. You sit back and watch the valley floor get small beneath you.

The landing is on the grassy field near the parking area. Soft, controlled. You unclip, hand the harness back, and that's it.

## What Makes It Worth Doing

I'll be direct with you. The flying itself is not the most dramatic paragliding you'll ever do. Solang is a valley thermal flight, not a high altitude ridge soaring experience. You don't get the kind of sustained lift you'd find in Bir Billing or the big Himalayan passes. The flight is relatively short.

So why do it?

The view. Specifically, the view of the [Pir Panjal Range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pir_Panjal_Range) from the air. From the ground, the mountains around Solang look big but distant. From a thousand feet up, they surround you. You see the snow line, the tree line, the river cutting through the valley floor. You see how the whole landscape connects. That perspective is hard to get any other way in Manali unless you're willing to trek for days.

The other reason is the morning light. If you go early, before 9am, the sun hits the eastern face of the mountains and the whole valley glows. The air is clear. The wind is calm. The crowds haven't arrived yet. That first hour of the day in Solang feels like a different place entirely.

Also, the landing field is right next to the river. After you land, you can walk over, sit on a rock, and watch the next group come down. It's a pleasant way to spend a morning.

## Practical Notes

**Go early.** Before 9am. I cannot stress this enough. By 10am, the parking lot is full, the queue for the jeep ride up is long, and the wind gets inconsistent. The pilots prefer the early window too. Better thermals, safer conditions, shorter waits.

**Winter is different.** Solang Valley paragliding is a summer and autumn activity. In winter, the whole valley switches over to a small skiing setup. The paragliding operators pack up their gear and come back around March or April. If you're coming in January or February, don't plan on flying here.

**Check the conditions before you leave your hotel.** I run a live status page for this exact reason. You can [check live snow status](https://manali.today/) on the site, and it will tell you what the weather is doing in the valley right now. If it's raining in Manali, it's probably raining harder in Solang. Save yourself the drive.

**Fitness level.** You need to be able to run about ten steps on a grassy slope. That's it. The pilot does the heavy work. If you have mobility issues, talk to the operator beforehand. Some of them can arrange an easier launch point.

**What to wear.** Closed shoes. A light jacket, even in summer. The air is cold at altitude, and the windchill during the flight is real. Sunglasses. No loose scarves or hats that could blow away.

**Rohtang Pass connection.** If you're planning to go to Rohtang Pass after Solang, keep an eye on the road conditions. The pass opens and closes based on weather and maintenance. I update the [check live Rohtang Pass status](https://manali.today/is-rohtang-pass-open-today) page every morning. Check it before you plan your day.

## Who Should Skip This

Not everyone needs to do Solang Valley paragliding. Here's who I'd tell to skip it.

People who are afraid of heights. I know that sounds obvious, but I've watched tourists freeze at the launch point and refuse to run. You lose your money and feel embarrassed. If you know you don't like heights, don't convince yourself this time will be different.

People who want a long flight. 8 to 15 minutes is not a lot of airtime. If you're a serious paraglider or someone who wants to spend half an hour floating, go to [Bir Billing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bir,_Himachal_Pradesh) instead. Solang is a short taste, not a full meal.

People who don't care about adventure activities at all. Solang Valley is worth half a day if adventure activities matter to your group. If they don't, skip it. The valley itself is scenic but not dramatically different from other spots closer to Manali. You won't miss anything essential.

People on a tight schedule. If you only have one full day in Manali and you're not specifically interested in paragliding, spend your time elsewhere. Walk the old village. Visit the temples. Sit by the river. That's a better use of a single day than driving to Solang, waiting around, flying for ten minutes, and driving back.
