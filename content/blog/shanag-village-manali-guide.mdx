I've been in Old Manali since 2018. I run manali.today because someone has to tell people what the roads and snow actually look like before they pack their bags. Every season, I watch tourists roll into town and book rooms on Mall Road or near the bridge in Siyal, then spend their whole trip stuck in traffic jams and honking. They message me asking if there's a way out. There is. It's called Shanag.

## What Shanag Village Actually Is

Shanag sits on the slopes above the main valley. It's quiet up there. You can hear the river from a distance, but you don't hear the constant rev of taxis and buses fighting for space on the main road. The village itself is small. A handful of guesthouses, some apple orchards, a few families who have been here longer than any of us have been alive. The broader [Kullu district](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kullu_district) is known for its apple orchards and mountain villages like this one.

The key thing about Shanag is the balance. You're not isolated. You can drive down into town for a meal or to buy supplies without living in the congestion every single minute of your trip. I've watched families check into places on Mall Road and within an hour the kids are restless because there's nowhere to just be. In Shanag, the kids can run around outside. The parents can sit on a balcony and look at the mountains without a car horn interrupting.

I recommend Shanag specifically to families over Mall Road, Nasogi, or Siyal. Those areas put you in the thick of peak season traffic the entire trip. You didn't come to Manali to sit in a queue of rental cars. You came for the mountains. Shanag gives you the mountains with the convenience of town nearby.

## Why I'd Send You Here (or Not)

Here's the honest take. If your idea of a Manali trip involves jumping from café to café on Mall Road, buying woolens at every shop, and being in the middle of the noise, stay in town. Shanag will frustrate you. You'll feel like you're missing the action.

But if you're coming with family, or you're a couple who actually wants to sit still for a few days, Shanag is the right call. I've seen too many people burn out on their Manali trip because they stayed somewhere that never let them rest. The valley is busy. That's just the reality of it now. But you don't have to live in the middle of the busy.

The best way to pick a stay in Shanag is to pair it with checking for a nearby meadow or a short easy hike before you book. Not every guesthouse has good access to a walking trail. Some are just rooms on a slope. Ask the owner directly. If they can't point you to a trail within a ten minute walk, keep looking.

For the full picture of where else to stay and what to avoid, I wrote [the complete Manali travel guide](https://manali.today/guide/manali) that covers the whole valley. Shanag is one option. It's not the only good one, but for families it's the one I keep coming back to.

## What to Actually Do There

Shanag doesn't have a list of attractions. That's the point. You go there to do less.

Walk the village roads. They're narrow and unpaved in places. The apple trees are everywhere. If you come in autumn, the fruit hangs heavy and the owners don't mind if you pick one off the ground. In spring, the blossoms make the whole hillside look soft and white.

There's a short trail that starts near the upper edge of the village and cuts across the slope toward a small meadow. It's not a famous trail. It doesn't have a name on any map. But it's the kind of walk that reminds you why you came to the mountains. The ground is uneven. The air smells of pine and damp earth. You can sit on a rock and watch the clouds move across the valley.

If you want a proper marked trail, the walk from Rocky's Cafe in Old Manali up to the meadows is a good option. I wrote about it in [The Three Meadows Trail From Rocky's Cafe in Old Manali](https://manali.today/blog/rockys-cafe-meadows-trek-old-manali). It's a few kilometers from Shanag, but the drive down is short.

For the snow chasers, Shanag gets its own layer of white when the winter storms roll in. It's not Rohtang levels of snow, but the village looks good in winter. You can [check live snow status](https://manali.today/) on the site to see what the coverage looks like before you book.

## Practical Notes

Shanag is about three kilometers from the main Mall Road junction. The road up is steep in parts and narrow. If you're driving a big SUV, take it slow. If you're taking a taxi, agree on the fare before you leave town. Some drivers will try to charge extra for the climb.

There is no ATM in Shanag. No grocery store worth mentioning. Stock up on snacks and cash before you head up. The guesthouses usually offer meals, but the menu is whatever the cook decided to make that day. That's part of the charm if you're flexible, and a problem if you're picky.

Winter access is fine as long as the roads are cleared. The municipal snow clearing is reliable on the main roads. The smaller lanes can stay icy for days. If you're coming between December and February, check the conditions. The [India Meteorological Department](https://mausam.imd.gov.in/) provides weather updates that can help you plan for winter travel. I update [check live Atal Tunnel status](https://manali.today/is-atal-tunnel-open-today) and [check live Rohtang Pass status](https://manali.today/is-rohtang-pass-open-today) every day so you know what you're driving into.

One thing I'll say plainly. Don't come to Shanag in the monsoon months. The road gets slippery. The landslides on the highway make it hard to get here in the first place. I wrote about this in [Why I Tell People to Skip Manali in the Monsoon Window](https://manali.today/blog/manali-monsoon-landslide-season-guide). July and August are not the time for this village.

If you're coming for the first snowfall of the season, read [First Snowfall of the Season in Manali: What It Actually Means for Your Trip](https://manali.today/blog/first-snowfall-manali-2026-2027-season). That post tells you what to expect when the first big storm hits. It changes everything about how you move around the valley.

## A Final Word

I don't get paid to recommend Shanag. I don't own a guesthouse there. I just see the same mistakes every season and I'd rather you didn't make them. Manali is a beautiful place, but it's also a crowded one. The trick is finding the corner that lets you breathe. For a lot of people, that corner is Shanag. Come see it for yourself. Bring good walking shoes and a willingness to sit still. That's all you need.
