I get messages every week from people who've booked their Manali trip based on Instagram reels. They send me screenshots of snow-covered slopes, blue skies, and happy couples in matching snowsuits. Then they ask: "Is Rohtang going to look like this when I go?"

The short answer is: probably not. But that doesn't mean you shouldn't go. It means you should go with the right expectations.

Let me walk you through what Rohtang Pass actually looks like, how to handle the altitude, what to do about gear, and when to go so you're not disappointed.

## The Snow Situation

The photos you see online are usually taken in one of two windows: right after the pass opens in March or April, or during a fresh snowfall in late August. Outside those windows, the snow at the main tourist spots gets trampled by thousands of people every day. It turns grey. It gets packed down into hard, dirty ice. The pristine white carpet you imagined is more of a slushy parking lot near the rental stalls.

That's the honest truth. I've seen people step off their taxi, look at the ground, and ask their driver if this is the right place.

But here's the thing: if you're willing to walk a little further from the main crowds, you'll find cleaner snow. The problem is most people don't. They stay within fifty meters of where the taxi drops them, take their photos with the dirty snow in the background, and leave thinking Rohtang is overrated.

The best time to visit Rohtang Pass for good snow conditions is either early season (March to mid-April) or right after a fresh dump in late August or September. Outside those windows, adjust your expectations. The experience is still worth it. The views of the surrounding peaks are incredible regardless. Just don't expect the Instagram version. For more on the geography and history of the pass, you can check the [Wikipedia article on Rohtang Pass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rohtang_Pass).

## The Altitude Is Not a Joke

Rohtang Pass sits at 3,978 meters. That's over 13,000 feet. Most visitors come from the plains: Delhi, Mumbai, Punjab, places near sea level. Your body has never experienced altitude like this. You don't know how it will react until you're there.

People over 45 are especially at risk for Acute Mountain Sickness. I've seen otherwise healthy men in their fifties get hit hard. They feel nauseous, get headaches, and sometimes panic because they don't understand what's happening.

Here's what works:

Drink water. Lots of it. Start the morning before you leave Manali and keep drinking throughout the day. Dehydration makes altitude sickness worse.

Carry AMS medicine. You can buy it at any pharmacy in Manali. Take it before you feel sick, or at the first sign of symptoms.

If you feel nausea or trouble breathing, take the medicine and keep walking around slowly. Do not sit in one place. Do not lie down. And absolutely do not run or exert yourself. Slow, steady movement helps your body adjust. Sitting still makes the symptoms worse.

If symptoms get severe, head back down immediately. There's no shame in turning around. The mountain will be there another day. The [Britannica entry on altitude sickness](https://www.britannica.com/science/altitude-sickness) provides more detail on how the condition affects the body.

## What About Snow Gear?

This is where the FOMO game gets played hard. Vendors in Manali will tell you that snow gear and equipment won't be available at the top. They'll pressure you to rent snowsuits, boots, and gloves before you leave town. They'll say you'll freeze without them.

Don't fall for it.

Everything you need is available at the last point before Rohtang. The rental stalls there have snowsuits, gum boots, gloves, and goggles. Renting up there beats hiking around sweaty in a snowsuit you picked up in Manali. You'll be carrying the extra weight up the mountain for no reason.

One practical tip: the gum boots at the top are basic but they work. Your regular sports shoes will get soaked within five minutes. Rent the boots. Your feet will thank you.

## The Activities and the Prices

Snow activities at Rohtang are a free market with no rules. Snowmobiling, ATV rides, sledding, snow tubing, horse rides. The prices are heavily inflated. Vendors start high because they expect you to haggle. So haggle. Hard. Don't pay the first price quoted. Walk away if it feels too high. They'll call you back.

But here's the more important point: none of these activities are properly regulated by the local government. Accidents happen. Every season I hear about someone getting hurt on a snowmobile or a horse. Use your own judgment before signing up for anything. Look at the equipment. Is the snowmobile beat up? Are the ropes frayed? Does the operator look like he's been drinking? If something feels off, skip it.

The sledding and tubing are generally safer. The motorized stuff carries more risk. For official information on permits and regulations, visit the [Himachal Pradesh tourism website](https://himachaltourism.gov.in).

## Before You Go

Check the [live Rohtang Pass status](https://manali.today/is-rohtang-pass-open-today) before you leave your hotel. The pass closes unpredictably due to weather, snow clearance, or landslides. Nothing ruins a day faster than driving two hours only to find the gate closed.

Also check the [live Atal Tunnel status](https://manali.today/is-atal-tunnel-open-today) if you're planning to go beyond Rohtang into Lahaul. The tunnel has its own schedule and closures.

And if you're wondering whether the snow is actually worth seeing on any given day, [check the live snow status](https://manali.today/) on the site. I update it myself based on what I see and what my contacts report.

## The Bottom Line

Rohtang Pass is not a disappointment if you know what you're getting into. Go early in the season or after fresh snow for the best conditions. Rent your gear at the top. Drink water. Carry AMS medicine. Haggle on prices. Use your judgment on activities. And check the live status before you head out.

The pass has been a crossing point for centuries. People have moved through it long before Instagram made it famous. It deserves more than a rushed photo and a complaint about dirty snow. Go with open eyes, and you might actually enjoy it.
